The morning broke with an air of grim finality, the **valley of ashes** shrouded in a pale mist that clung to everything like the residue of sorrow. I stood at the window of Gatsby’s mansion, surveying the remnants of his dream—the sprawling lawn, still damp from last night’s rain, the empty pool whose still waters held the faint memory of tragedy, and the house itself, an extravagant skeleton now bereft of purpose. A silence had descended upon West Egg, the kind that settles in the wake of calamity, heavy and unyielding.

The phone rang faintly in the distance, its tinny echo traveling through the cavernous halls, but I did not move to answer it. Gatsby was gone, and with him the frenetic energy that had animated this place, transforming it into a haven for the restless and disillusioned. Now, the rooms were like mausoleums, each containing fragments of a life that had burned too brightly. The air was thick with the scent of wilted flowers, remnants of the **lavish parties** that once spilled out onto the terraces, their gaiety now a cruel mockery of what had transpired.

I spent the day making arrangements, my mind a machine grinding through the logistics of death. There was no one to share the burden—no family to console, no friends to rally. Gatsby, for all his wealth and charm, had lived in the shadows of others, his existence tethered to their whims and desires. Daisy was unreachable, her name a ghost that lingered in the corridors of my thoughts. She and Tom had vanished, their retreat as sudden and thoughtless as their arrival into Gatsby’s life, leaving behind the wreckage they had wrought.

I called **Meyer Wolfsheim**, hoping to draw some semblance of humanity from the man who had once boasted of his connection to Gatsby. His voice, when it came, was clipped and dispassionate. “I can’t get mixed up in it,” he said, his words as cold as the marble floors beneath my feet. “He was a good fellow, Nick, but my hands are tied.” The call ended abruptly, and I was left staring at the receiver, its lifeless form mocking my futile efforts.

By late afternoon, the house began to fill with strangers. The coroner arrived first, his briefcase clinking ominously as he passed me in the hall. Then the reporters came, their pens darting across their notepads like scavengers picking at a carcass. They spoke in hushed tones, their questions laced with insinuation, eager to unravel the mystery of Gatsby’s life and death. I answered as best I could, though their interest in truth seemed secondary to their hunger for scandal. They lingered in the foyer, their eyes darting to the grand staircase and the crystal chandeliers, as if the opulence might yield some hidden clue.

The funeral itself was a bleak affair, attended by a handful of Gatsby’s staff and myself. I had sent invitations far and wide, hoping against hope that someone—anyone—might come to honor him. But the replies were few and perfunctory, each a variation of the same polite refusal. Even **Klipspringer**, who had spent so many nights playing the piano in Gatsby’s parlor, declined to attend, citing a prior engagement. “I’ve got to attend a luncheon,” he explained over the phone, his tone apologetic but firm. “You understand, don’t you?” I hung up without replying, my disgust too profound for words.

The minister, an elderly man whose voice wavered like a trembling wick, spoke briefly of eternity and redemption, his platitudes falling flat against the weight of Gatsby’s absence. The coffin was lowered into the earth, its descent marked by the dull thud of soil striking wood. I stood alone at the gravesite, the wind picking up around me, carrying with it the faint scent of salt from the **Long Island Sound**. The others had departed as quickly as they had arrived, their presence fleeting and impersonal, a stark contrast to the man whose life we were meant to honor.

Afterward, I returned to the mansion, its grandeur now a hollow echo of its former self. The rooms seemed larger somehow, their emptiness magnified by the absence of the man who had filled them with his ambition and hope. I wandered through the halls, my footsteps muffled by the thick carpeting, until I found myself in Gatsby’s library. The books, untouched and pristine, stared back at me from their shelves, their gilded spines a testament to the illusion of substance. I pulled one out at random, its pages crisp and unmarked, and thought of the man who had amassed these treasures not for their content but for the image they projected.

It was here, surrounded by the remnants of Gatsby’s dream, that the weight of it all came crashing down. His life—a mosaic of contradictions and artifice—had been built on the fragile foundation of hope, and when that hope was extinguished, so too was the man himself. He had chased Daisy with a fervor that bordered on mania, convinced that she was the key to his salvation, the embodiment of everything he had ever desired. But Daisy was no goddess, no shimmering beacon of perfection; she was flesh and blood, flawed and fickle, her charm a veneer that masked the emptiness beneath. Gatsby had gambled everything on a dream that was never his to claim, and in the end, it had destroyed him.

I left West Egg shortly thereafter, returning to the Midwest where the rhythm of life was slower, its ambitions less consuming. The East had lost its allure, its promises of wealth and glamour revealed to be nothing more than a mirage. The people I had met—the Buchanans, Wolfsheim, Klipspringer—were shadows of humanity, their lives defined by greed and selfishness, their loyalties as transient as the seasons. And Gatsby, for all his flaws, had been the exception—a man whose capacity for hope exceeded the bounds of reason, whose belief in the possibility of happiness was both his greatest strength and his ultimate undoing.

As the train carried me away from the glittering wasteland of the East, I thought of the **green light**, that faint and distant beacon that had drawn Gatsby across the divide of years and circumstance. It had been his guiding star, the symbol of a future filled with promise and love. But the light was an illusion, its glow a cruel trick played by time and fate. Gatsby’s quest, noble as it was, had been doomed from the start, a collision between the ideal and the real that could only end in heartbreak.

And yet, as I watched the fields and rivers blur past the window, I could not help but feel a pang of longing—for the man who had dared to dream, who had sought to transcend the limitations of his past and create a new world. Gatsby was gone, his mansion empty, his dream shattered. But his story remained, a testament to the power of hope and the tragedy of ambition, a reminder that even in the darkest of times, there is beauty in the attempt.
