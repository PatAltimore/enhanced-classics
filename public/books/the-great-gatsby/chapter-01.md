In my younger and more vulnerable years my father gave me some advice that I've been turning over in my mind ever since. "Whenever you feel like criticizing any one," he told me, "just remember that all the people in this world haven't had the advantages that you've had."

Only Gatsby, the man who gives his name to this book, was exempt from my reaction — Gatsby who represented everything for which I have an unaffected scorn. No, Gatsby turned out all right at the end; it is what preyed on Gatsby, what foul dust floated in the wake of his dreams that temporarily closed out my interest in the abortive sorrows and short-winded elations of men. He stood for me, in the end, a particular version of the **American Dream** — the belief that reinvention was always possible, that the past could be left behind, that enough money and enough will could purchase a second chance.

My family have been prominent, well-to-do people in this Middle Western city for three generations. After the war I decided to go East and learn the **bond business**. Everyone I knew was in the bond business, so I supposed it could support one more single man. All my aunts and uncles talked it over as if they were choosing a prep school for me, and finally said, "Why — yes," with very grave, hesitant faces.

It was 1922, two years into **Prohibition** and deep in the decade they were already calling the **Jazz Age**. I rented a house in **West Egg** — the less fashionable of the two egg-shaped peninsulas, though this is a most superficial tag to express the bizarre and not a little sinister contrast between them. My house was at the tip of the egg, only fifty yards from the Sound, squeezed between two huge places that rented for twelve or fifteen hundred a season.

Across the courtesy bay the white palaces of fashionable East Egg glittered along the water, and the history of the summer really begins on the evening I drove over there to have dinner with the Tom Buchanans. The water of **Long Island Sound** lay calm between the two shores, reflecting the last of the afternoon light.

Daisy was my second cousin once removed, and I'd known Tom in college. His family were enormously wealthy — even in college his freedom with money was a matter for reproach — and he'd left Chicago and come East in a fashion that rather took your breath away. Why he had come East I don't know. He had brought down a string of polo ponies from Lake Forest. It was hard to realize that a man in my own generation was wealthy enough to do that.

Tom Buchanan had been one of the most powerful ends that ever played football at **Yale** — a national figure in a way, one of those men who reach such an acute limited excellence at twenty-one that everything afterwards savours of anti-climax. Now, at thirty, his acquaintances still detested him. His speaking voice was a gruff, husky tenor that added to the impression of fractiousness he conveyed.

The other girl, Daisy said, was **Jordan Baker**. I knew her name — I'd seen it in a sporting photograph — though I couldn't recall just where. I knew now why her face was familiar — its pleasing contemptuous expression had looked out at me from many rotogravure pictures of the sporting life at Asheville and Hot Springs and Palm Beach. She was a slender, small-breasted girl, with an erect carriage, which she accentuated by throwing her body backward at the shoulders like a young cadet.

Tom excused himself and went inside. The telephone rang in the hall. Daisy leaned forward with a bright look and a small laugh. Jordan Baker looked at her, then at me, and smiled. I didn't say anything.

"You live in West Egg," said Jordan to me. "I know somebody there." She turned away. It was already on everyone's tongue: the parties, the lights, the man in the white flannel suit who stood at the end of his dock in the dark.

It was nine o'clock when I walked down to the water's edge. On the Sound, a single light moved in the dark — a boat running without headlamps. There was music from my neighbor's house through the summer nights. In his blue gardens men and girls came and went like moths among the whisperings and the champagne and the stars.

A figure had detached itself from the shadow of the mansion next door and was standing on the dock, alone. He stretched his arms out toward the dark water in a trembling way, and, far away, I could see nothing except a single **green light**, minute and far away, that might have been the end of a dock.

When I looked once more for Gatsby he had vanished, and I was alone again in the unquiet darkness.
