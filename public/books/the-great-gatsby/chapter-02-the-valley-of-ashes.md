About halfway between West Egg and New York the motor road hastily joins the railroad and runs beside it for a quarter of a mile, so as to shrink away from a certain desolate area of land. This is a **valley of ashes**—a fantastic farm where ashes grow like wheat into ridges and hills and grotesque gardens; where ashes take the forms of houses and chimneys and rising smoke and, finally, with a transcendent effort, of men who move dimly and already crumbling through the powdery air. Occasionally a line of gray cars crawls along an invisible track, gives out a ghastly creak, and comes to rest, and immediately the ash-gray men swarm up with leaden spades and stir up an impenetrable cloud, which screens their obscure operations from your sight. 

But above the gray land and the spasms of bleak dust which drift endlessly over it, you perceive, after a moment, the eyes of **Doctor T. J. Eckleburg**. The eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg are blue and gigantic—their retinas are one yard high. They look out of no face, but, instead, from a pair of enormous yellow spectacles which pass over a nonexistent nose. Evidently some wild wag of an oculist set them there to fatten his practice in the borough of Queens, and then sank down himself into eternal blindness, or forgot them and moved away. But his eyes, dimmed a little by many paintless days under sun and rain, brood on over the solemn dumping ground.

The valley of ashes is bounded on one side by a small foul river, and, when the drawbridge is up to let barges through, the passengers on waiting trains can stare at the dismal scene for as long as half an hour. There is always a halt there of at least a minute, and it was because of this that I first met **Tom Buchanan’s mistress**.

The fact that he had one was insisted upon wherever he was known. His acquaintances resented the fact that he turned up in popular restaurants with her and, leaving her at a table, sauntered about chatting with whomsoever he knew. Though I was curious to see her, I had no desire to meet her—but I did. I went up to New York with Tom on the train one afternoon, and when we stopped by the ashheaps he jumped to his feet and, taking hold of my elbow, literally forced me from the car. 

“We’re getting off,” he insisted. “I want you to meet my girl.”

I followed him over a low whitewashed railroad fence, and we walked a hundred yards along the road under the **Doctor Eckleburg’s persistent stare**, which seemed to follow us and to shrink and flinch from Tom’s harsh glances. 

The gray land gave way to an ochre and brown dust, and Tom led me briskly to a **garage**, where a set of rickety stairs descended into the earthy gloom of an office. The interior was unprosperous and bare; the only car visible was the dust-covered wreck of a Ford which crouched in a dim corner. It had been pushed there deliberately, perhaps, or was simply abandoned.

A blond, spiritless man, anaemic, and faintly handsome, stood in the doorway of the office. He was **George B. Wilson**, the proprietor of the garage, who seemed to be more a ghost of a man than a living one.

When he saw us, his expression changed for an instant into an eager brightness, but Tom Buchanan’s glance seemed to cage him back into his habitual state of vague desperation. Tom regarded him with contemptuous interest. 

“Hello, Wilson, old man,” he said, slapping the man on the shoulder. “How’s business?”

“I can’t complain,” Wilson said unconvincingly. “When are you going to sell me that car?”

“Next week; I’ve got my man working on it now.” 

“Works pretty slow, don’t he?”

“No, he’s all right, though I can’t reach him. I’m afraid I’ll have to get a new man.”

Wilson’s face fell. He seemed to lose himself into the garage once more, shrinking away from Tom’s commanding presence. Then Tom glanced sharply at me and nodded toward a dusty window, where the figure of a woman moved indolently back and forth as if she were smearing the panes in the dull light.

“Let’s go up,” Tom said. 

We walked through the office and up the rickety stairs. Myrtle Wilson, his mistress, was lounging on a couch, dressed in a brown figured muslin, her face above the spotted dress glowing with vitality. She was in her **middle thirties**, and faintly stout, but she carried her surplus flesh sensuously, as some women can. Her face contained no facet or gleam of beauty, but there was an immediately perceptible vitality about her as if the nerves of her body were continually smouldering.

She smiled slowly and, walking through her husband as if he were a ghost, shook hands with Tom, looked him flush in the eye, and then with a flutter of her lashes and a nod toward the garage, said:

“Get some chairs, why don’t you, so somebody can sit down.”

“Oh, sure,” agreed Wilson hurriedly, and went toward the little office, mingling immediately with the ash-gray men.

Tom Buchanan yanked the curtains aside and, with a wave of his hand, ordered the room to be aired. Myrtle threw herself down on the couch, and the warm breath of her voice sank into the room as she murmured:

“I can’t stand this place. Let’s get out.”

“Come on,” said Tom quickly. “I want you to meet someone.”

We drove over to New York in Tom’s blue coupé, and Myrtle sat in the front seat, her laughter and chatter filling the car like a burst of life amidst the dead world of the valley. She directed Tom to stop at a newsstand, where she bought a copy of **Town Tattle** and some magazines, flicking through them with an air of great importance as she settled into her role of queenly indulgence.

At the station in New York, Myrtle made Tom buy her a small dog, a **tiny police dog**, whose eyes gleamed with nervous energy. She carried it in her arms, cooing over it as if it were the central object of her affections, while Tom, irritated but indulgent, paid the vendor and ushered us into a waiting taxicab. 

We rode through the streets until we arrived at a building marked by a faded yellow brick and a **third-floor apartment**, where Tom and Myrtle apparently conducted their affair away from prying eyes. The room was crowded with overstuffed furniture, tapestries, and gaudy lampshades, all of which seemed to vibrate with Myrtle’s personality, transforming the dingy flat into a shrine of her aspirations.

A bottle of whiskey appeared from nowhere, and soon the room swelled with voices as Myrtle invited neighbors and acquaintances for an impromptu gathering. Among them was **Catherine**, her sister, a slender worldly girl with a powdered complexion and a smirk that suggested she knew far more than she let on. As the whiskey flowed, the conversation grew louder, filled with half-truths and boastful exaggerations. 

Catherine leaned forward confidentially, her voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper as she discussed Tom and Myrtle’s relationship. She asserted that neither Tom nor Myrtle were happy in their marriages and hinted darkly at the idea that they could marry each other once Tom divorced Daisy—though she seemed oblivious to the improbability of such a claim.

The party grew louder, the air thick with smoke and the heavy scent of perfume, and Myrtle’s laughter rang out above it all, shrill and commanding. She grew more theatrical as the evening wore on, recounting tales of her dissatisfaction with George and declaring that she had made a mistake in marrying him. 

“I thought he was a gentleman,” she said, her voice rising in indignation. “I thought he knew something about breeding, but he wasn’t fit to lick my shoe.”

Tom’s eyes flashed with irritation, but he said nothing, letting Myrtle dominate the room with her coarse vitality. The tension mounted, however, as Myrtle, emboldened by drink, began to taunt Tom about Daisy, repeating her name with a mocking lilt.

“Daisy! Daisy! Daisy!” she cried, her voice a sharp jab in the thick air. “I’ll say it whenever I want to! Daisy! Dai—”

With a sudden movement, Tom stepped forward and struck her across the face, silencing her with a brutal slap. The room fell silent, the air heavy with shock and the faint sound of Myrtle’s muffled sobs. 

No one spoke for a moment, and then the party resumed, but the mood had shifted irrevocably, the laughter forced and brittle. The gathering dissolved slowly, leaving behind the stale scent of whiskey and the faint echoes of Myrtle’s bruised pride.

I left the apartment with Tom, my mind reeling from the raw display of human desperation and cruelty I had witnessed. As we drove back through the **valley of ashes**, the eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg loomed above us, their gaze somber and unblinking, seeming to judge the broken lives that passed beneath them. In that moment, they appeared to hold all the secrets of the world, and yet they offered no answers—only the cold indifference of a silent sentinel.
