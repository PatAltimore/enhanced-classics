I remained for a time at the side of the sphinx, half hidden in the bushes, and watched the bronze pedestal of my **Time Machine**. I had made sure in the first moments of returning to this distant future that it was gone, and now I waited, as though its absence might resolve itself into some mistake or illusion. But no trick of the eye or lapse of reason could alter the cold facts before me. The pedestal was empty; the machine had vanished. My heart sank with the realization that my only means of returning to my own century had been taken from me.

The thought of immediate pursuit crossed my mind, but I was ill-equipped for such action. The **Eloi**, gentle and indolent as they were, could offer no assistance. Their faces, serene in their childish simplicity, betrayed no understanding of my plight. I questioned them, gesturing toward the pedestal and miming the motion of the machine, but they stared at me in incomprehension, their smiles unbroken by any shadow of concern. Their lack of curiosity, their inability to grasp the magnitude of my loss, filled me with a sudden frustration. It was as though I had been cast among **creatures of an entirely different order**, a race for whom urgency and ambition were foreign concepts.

I turned away from their blank innocence and began to search the area, my steps quickened by rising anxiety. The sun was low in the sky, and the long shadows stretched over the ruins of a civilization that seemed to mock my predicament. I examined the structure of the bronze pedestal more closely. It was adorned with intricate carvings, the artistry of which suggested a bygone era of considerable skill. Yet the pedestal itself was sealed, its surface unbroken by any crack or hinge. Clearly, the **Morlocks**, those subterranean dwellers who I had glimpsed only fleetingly, had a hand in this. The thought of venturing into their domain filled me with dread, but necessity overruled fear.

I decided to wait until nightfall before making any attempt to explore the underworld. The **sunset**, with its crimson and gold hues, seemed to mock my predicament, painting the sky in a palette of ostentatious beauty while I wrestled with despair. I sat among the ruins and contemplated the situation. The Eloi, with their fragile forms and playful gestures, were as far removed from the Morlocks as light is from darkness. Yet the two races were bound together in some grim symbiosis, an arrangement that I had yet to understand fully. My mind wandered back to the theories of **social evolution**, theories that once seemed abstract and speculative. Here, in this strange world, the principles of class division and survival had taken on a chilling reality.

When the last rays of light faded and the stars emerged, I steeled myself for the descent. I carried a small box of **matches**, one of the few items I had brought with me, and felt their reassuring weight in my pocket. The thought of fire brought some comfort amid the oppressive darkness that awaited me. The Eloi scattered as the night deepened, retreating to their shelters with the instinctive fear of the coming shadows. Their behavior confirmed my suspicions: the night belonged to the Morlocks.

I made for the nearest entrance to their underground lair, a shadowy shaft I had glimpsed earlier in the day. Its mouth gaped like a wound in the earth, and I hesitated at its edge, peering into the gloom below. The air that rose from the depths was heavy and stale, carrying with it a faint, metallic tang. The faint sound of machinery reached my ears, a distant hum that hinted at the **industrial activity** hidden beneath the surface. It struck me as a grotesque inversion of the Eloi's world above, a place of toil and darkness beneath a realm of leisure and light.

Gripping the matches tightly, I began my descent. The walls of the shaft were rough and uneven, forcing me to climb carefully, lest I lose my footing and plunge into the abyss. The darkness closed in around me, and with each step downward, the sense of claustrophobia grew stronger. At last, my feet touched a solid surface, and I struck a match, its small flame casting flickering shadows on the walls. The sight that greeted me was both astonishing and horrifying.

The underground world of the Morlocks was vast and labyrinthine, a network of tunnels and chambers that extended beyond the reach of the feeble light. The walls were lined with machinery, their cogs and pistons moving with relentless precision. The Morlocks themselves, pale and sinewy, moved among the machines in a manner that was both deliberate and furtive. Their eyes, reflecting the flame of my match, glowed with an eerie luminescence. They seemed startled by my presence, their movements halting for a moment before resuming their work. I realized at once that I was an intruder in their domain, an unwelcome guest in a world built on secrecy and shadow.

I ventured further, my steps cautious, my eyes scanning every corner of the dimly lit space. The Morlocks avoided me, retreating into the shadows whenever I drew near. Their behavior was unsettling, their silence more threatening than any overt aggression. I had the sense that they were observing me, waiting for the right moment to act. The hum of the machinery grew louder as I approached what seemed to be the central chamber, a vast space filled with whirring gears and pulsing lights. It was here that I found the **Time Machine**, standing amidst the chaos like an artifact of another age.

Relief flooded through me at the sight of the machine, but it was tempered by the realization that retrieving it would not be easy. The Morlocks had clearly taken great care to place it here, surrounded by their machinery and guarded by their presence. I struck another match, the flare of light drawing their attention. Their pale faces turned toward me, their eyes narrowing in what I could only interpret as hostility. My heart raced as I considered my options. I would have to act quickly, for the match was already burning low.

With a sudden burst of courage, I dashed toward the Time Machine, the flame of the match flickering wildly as I moved. The Morlocks reacted at once, their movements swift and coordinated. They closed in around me, their hands reaching out with an almost mechanical precision. I swung the matchbox wildly, the light startling them and forcing them to retreat momentarily. It was in this chaotic struggle that I realized the extent of their fear of fire, their aversion to the light that illuminated their dark world.

I reached the machine and grasped its controls, my hands trembling as I adjusted the levers. The Morlocks pressed closer, their numbers overwhelming, their presence oppressive. With a final, desperate motion, I activated the machine, its mechanisms springing to life with a familiar hum. The chamber dissolved into a blur of light and motion as the Time Machine carried me away from the subterranean horrors, away from the reach of the Morlocks. My last sight of them was a mass of pale faces, their expressions twisted in anger and confusion as the light engulfed them.

And then, silence. The machine came to a halt, and I found myself in another time, another place. The stars above were unfamiliar, the landscape alien. But I was alive, and the machine was intact. My heart was pounding, my mind racing with the memory of what I had just experienced. The division between the Eloi and the Morlocks, the darkness beneath the surface, the machinery that symbolized a world of toil and oppression—all of it lingered in my thoughts as I prepared to face whatever new challenges awaited me in this distant future.
