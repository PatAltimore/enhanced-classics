The year 1866 was signalized by a remarkable incident, a mysterious and inexplicable phenomenon, which doubtless no one has yet forgotten. Not to mention the rumors which agitated the maritime populations and excited the public mind, even in the interior of continents — seafaring men were particularly disturbed. Merchants, captains, skippers, and master mariners of Europe and America; naval officers of all countries; and the governments of several states on two continents were deeply interested in the matter.

For some months past, vessels had been met by an enormous thing — a long object, spindle-shaped, occasionally **phosphorescent**, and infinitely larger and more rapid in its movements than a whale. The facts relating to this apparition, as entered in various log-books, agreed in most respects as to the shape of the object, the undeniable swiftness of its movements, its surprising power of locomotion, and the peculiar life with which it seemed endowed.

On the 13th of April, 1867, the **Scotia**, one of the finest vessels in the Cunard fleet, found herself, at 15° 12' west longitude and 45° 37' north latitude, struck on her port side by an object she could not identify. When her engineers examined the hull in dry dock at Liverpool, they found a regular cone-shaped hole, punched through iron plating a full inch and a quarter thick. The puncture was so clean, so precise, that a sword could scarcely have done it better. Whatever had struck the Scotia from below had done so with the force of an engine and the geometry of a drill.

Whatever it was, it moved. The sightings covered the Pacific and Atlantic within weeks of each other, eliminating any geological explanation. A **shifting reef** could not cross an ocean. Nor could it generate light. Nor could it flee at the sight of a man-of-war. It was alive.

The question was what kind of alive. Some proposed a **sea serpent** — the ancient terror of sailors, dismissed by scientists for a generation and now reluctantly returning to the conversation. Others, more rigorous, calculated the force required to pierce the Scotia's hull and concluded that only a living spur of extraordinary density and mass could explain it. A **narwhal** of monstrous dimensions — fitted by nature with precisely such an ivory lance — was the most scientifically defensible hypothesis. But a narwhal a hundred feet long had never been observed by anyone living.

Others still, less committed to a single species, proposed simply a **giant squid** — the kraken of Norse legend, the creature that had been washing ashore in fragments for a century and being systematically ignored by respectable naturalists. By 1866, ignoring it was becoming difficult.

It was at this point that I, **Professor Aronnax** of the Paris Museum of Natural History, found myself drawn into the matter. I had returned from a long expedition in the territories of Nebraska, where I had been studying the natural history of the American West, to find Paris consumed by the controversy. I had published something on great marine creatures in the Revue des deux mondes, and I was asked, from several quarters, to give my opinion.

It was also the year, I should note, in which the **Great Eastern** completed the Atlantic telegraph cable — the year we became certain we had mastered the sea's surface. That mastery made what lay beneath it seem stranger by contrast. We had connected the continents with a wire laid across the ocean floor. We had not the faintest idea what was moving through the water three miles above it.

I had not yet formed a fixed opinion. I kept an open mind, which in science is not weakness but discipline. Something had struck the Scotia. Something had been seen, and tracked, and twice had outrun steam-powered vessels at full power. I was prepared to believe almost anything. What I was not prepared for was the truth.
